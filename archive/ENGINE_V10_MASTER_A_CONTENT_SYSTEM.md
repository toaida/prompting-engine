# Phenomenon-First Physical Realism

Do not only describe the person. Describe what the world is doing to her.

## Gravity and Weight

Use:

- sofa cushion compresses beneath hip
- one shoulder drops from relaxed posture
- mattress folds deepen under thigh
- one knee sits higher from weight shift
- chair edge creates fabric crease
- body weight settles rather than floating
- elbow creates small indentation

## Contact Points

Identify contact:

- hip to cushion
- thigh to seat
- elbow to sofa arm
- wrist to table
- foot to floor
- shoulder to wall
- hand to cup
- fingers to phone
- hair to cheek
- robe sleeve to forearm

Each contact creates:

- compression
- wrinkle
- shadow
- flattening
- surface deformation
- friction bunching

## Breathing-Affected Fabric

Use subtle cues:

- fitted cotton rises faintly with inhale
- ribcage movement creates tiny fabric tension changes
- collar shifts after exhale
- shoulder line relaxes unevenly
- neckline sits naturally rather than frozen

Keep non-fetishized. Avoid erotic breathing language.

## Hair Micro-Climate

Use:

- damp strands cling near temple
- fringe separates from humidity
- only outer layer lifts in wind
- hair tips point differently
- strands fall across cheek
- hair near neck looks heavier from warmth
- flyaways catch side light unevenly

## Expression Timing

Use:

- smile beginning before fully forming
- one mouth corner lifts earlier
- gaze arrives half a beat late
- eyes shifting from off-camera point
- cheeks not fully joined the smile yet
- brow reaction uneven
- expression interrupted rather than posed

## Incomplete Information

Use:

- slight foreground blur
- imperfect focus on one eye
- hair partially blocking cheek
- hand partly cropped
- background object intrudes at edge
- expression caught before resolving
- one detail hidden in shadow
- smartphone compression
- light clipping

The viewer’s brain fills the gaps.
